Abstract

The Sidepods function as essential elements in contemporary Formula One car design because they perform various engineering tasks under strict regulatory conditions. The sidepods which position between the front and rear wheels contain vital mechanical components while they serve as critical elements for both aerodynamic efficiency and driver protection. The research examines Formula One sidepod engineering functions, which include temperature management and air flow control and protection against side collisions. The research evaluates new technical regulations which include 2022 ground-effect rules to demonstrate how sidepod design has become more important in racing. The research investigates how sidepod designs impact complete vehicle performance and racing ability through an analysis of current racing data. 
        


        
        
Introduction

        
Formula One cars represent the most advanced racing vehicles which use their components to achieve peak performance while following all established rules. The sidepod section which runs between the front wheels and rear wheels serves as a fundamental component of the bodywork structure. The introduction of ground-effect regulations in 2022 has heightened the importance of sidepod design by eliminating several aerodynamic devices. The article examines Formula One sidepods from an engineering standpoint to understand their functions which include cooling systems and aerodynamic performance and protection of the driver. 

        


        
        
Analysis and Discussion


        
        
Sidepods are large aerodynamic bodywork on each side of a Formula One car. These are located between the front and rear wheels and attached to the main monocoque of the car. The key functions of sidepods are cooling components inside the chassis, managing the aerodynamics of airflow around and through the sidepod, and enhancing driver safety. Since 2022, Formula One regulations have made sidepod design even more critical. The new technical rules have increased the aerodynamic importance of sidepods by banning aerodynamic parts in this area, particularly the bargeboards (motorsportengineer.net, n.d).

One of the key purposes of the sidepods on a Formula One car is the cooling they provide for the components inside the car. This is essential as “With 1000hp on tap from the combustion engine and hybrid systems combined, an F1 car puts out an incredible amount of heat” (Scarborough, 2019).
“Sidepods contain radiators that dissipate engine heat, with coolant circulated by a water pump and regulated by a thermostat. Fans force air through the radiator to aid cooling” (motorsportengineer.net, n.d.). Air enters the sidepod inlet, flows through the radiator and heat exchangers, and the now-hot air exits at the back of the car without disturbing airflow. The size of the sidepod inlets changes based on race conditions. Larger inlets provide better cooling but increase drag, while smaller inlets reduce drag but may cause overheating. Tracks such as Singapore (due to its high track temperature) and Mexico City (due to its high altitude) require a larger inlet.

“The sidepods on an F1 car play a key role in the aerodynamics and downforce production capabilities of the cars. They are used to help generate some outwash and to manipulate the airflow over and to the back of the car. Many of the teams use wide sidepods to create a flow field going towards the rear tyres, which serves two purposes. Pushing the front wheel away from the car to avoid disturbing the aerodynamics at the rear and creating less pressure buildup at the rear tyres, resulting in less drag. This helps the cars maximise downforce production at the rear of the car, helping with traction and overall performance.” (Opong, 2023).
In addition to outwash effects, modern sidepods are designed to carefully manage airflow along the car's floor. The undercut section beneath the sidepod channels high-energy air towards the edges of the floor, helping to strengthen the ground-effect airflow beneath the car. “Undercuts on F1 sidepods are areas where the sidepods are cut out below the radiator intake, or towards the rear of the sidepod. This results in a shape where the sidepod is narrower towards the floor of the car, as if the underside of the sidepod has been ‘cut out’.” (Opong, 2023). This improves the diffuser's efficiency by maintaining low-pressure regions beneath the car, increasing downforce without adding significant drag.

Analysis and Discussion


        
        
The airflow around modern Formula One sidepods is divided between radiator cooling, underfloor venturi flow, and external bodywork, with these streams balanced to optimise cooling, drag, and downforce (Formula1.com, 2025; F1 Historian, n.d.; Motorsport Week, 2022).

Safety is a very important aspect of Formula One sidepods as they help to protect the driver from side impact crashes. Following high-profile accidents, Formula One regulations have increasingly focused on improving side impact protection. A major catalyst for change was Robert Kubica’s crash at the 2007 Canadian Grand Prix, which exposed weaknesses in the existing straight tube-based side impact structures when struck at an acute angle. As FIA Institute research consultant Andy Mellor explained, “We went back to basics to examine what a side impact structure really needs to do in different types of accident” (Mellor, 2013). 
The FIA implemented new side impact structures for the 2014 season because of these research results which transformed the way sidepods protect drivers. The new structures consist of laminated carbon fibre pillars designed to crush progressively under impact, rather than fracture or detach from the chassis. Unlike earlier team-specific designs, these side impact devices are now standardised components, ensuring consistent performance across all cars while still allowing teams flexibility in their integration within the sidepods. According to FIA Institute research, the redesign was intended to improve protection in impacts occurring at a wide range of angles, addressing the limitations revealed by earlier accidents (Mellor, 2013).

Recent Formula One seasons highlight how sidepod design influences overall car performance. Mercedes adopted a radical “zero pod” concept in 2022, featuring extremely narrow sidepods intended to reduce drag and maximise floor exposure. However, this design resulted in unstable airflow and inconsistent ground effect performance, leading the team to abandon the concept in later seasons. In contrast, Red Bull Racing employed wide, downwash-style sidepods that guide airflow towards the floor and diffuser, producing stable downforce and superior aerodynamic efficiency. This approach proved highly successful and was widely copied by rival teams (Somers, 2023).
